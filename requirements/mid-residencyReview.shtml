Requirements

Below, we state the course requirements for the College of Creative Studies Bachelor of Science in Computer Science. In addition to our B.S. program, we have a 5-year B.S.-M.S. Computer Science program, where the B.S. is from the College of Creative Studies and the M.S. is from the College of Engineering.

Courses

Mathematics

  • Math 5A-B -- 8 units
  • PSTAT 120A - 4 units

Science

Twenty units from the following list, including at least 1 laboratory course:

  • Physics: 1, 2, 3 & 3L, 4 & 4L, 5 & 5L
  • Chemistry: 1A & 1AL, 1B & 1BL, 1C & 1CL
  • Molecular, Cellular, & Developmental Biology: 1A & 1AL
  • Molecular, Cellular, & Developmental Biology: 1B & EEMB 2 & (1BL or 2L)

Computer Science

  • CCS CS 1A & L
  • CCS CS 2

They also are required to take those CCS computer science courses, and any other UCSB courses, that the student's faculty advisor deems necessary. This aspect of the curriculum is tailored for each student.

In the mathematics and science courses listed above, students may instead take a CCS course that their advisor deems acceptable. Some of the requirements above also can be met via advanced placement (please see the General Catalog).

Upper Division

The upper division requirements equal those for the B.S. in computer science in the College of Engineering. These include the Major requirements, the Major Field Electives, and the Ethics requirement. However, CCS students are not required to satisfy the formal prerequisites for the upper division courses that they take. Rather, they meet with their CCS faculty advisor each quarter, and obtain approval for their planned course of study for the next quarter.

Under exceptional circumstances, certain upper division CS course requirements may be waived. Examples of such circumstances include a case in which a student has completed advanced work or research in the topic of the course(s), or has arranged to take a more advanced course. All such waivers require written advisor approval.

Mid-Residency Review

The CCS/CS Program views the mid residency review as a mid-course adjustment, which provides the student with a bigger picture than quarterly individual advising provides. It is meant to give them a chance to report on their progress, reflect on what they have set out to do, what they have accomplished so far and where they want to go, and to make sure that their progress is aligned with the goals of the CCS Computer Science Program.

The procedure for the mid-residency review for CCS/CS students was determined in 2006 by a joint meeting of the faculty and CCS students. As a mid-residency review, an oral presentation by the student in which a majority of CCS CS faculty is present is conducted. The student's peers are invited to take part in the presentation, which should be about 20 minutes. CCS/CS students are expected to proceed with this review at the end of the fifth quarter of their studies in the CCS Computer Science program. The review takes place sometime towards the end of the finals week of the winter quarter of each year.

After the public part of the presentation, the faculty meet briefly to discuss the student's progress and to make recommendations. The faculty may choose to have an informal private meeting with the student. Based on the meeting and the ensuing discussion, the student is notified if there are concerns about progress and the appropriateness of the CCS Program for the student. If this is the case, specific advising guidelines and academic goals will be agreed upon in consultation with the student. Formal feedback to the student is provided and a written memorandum is placed in the student's file with a copy to the student.

CCS students felt that informal feedback from peers and during the public part of the review would be invaluable. No written comments from the student's peers will be solicited, as some issues were voiced as to the appropriateness of this.
